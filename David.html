David's Mighty Men

Role: Creative Director, UI Designer, Programmer
Duration: September 2025 – May 2026 (WIP)
Genre: 3D Hack N’ Slash Historical
Engine: Unreal Engine

David’s Mighty Men is a 3D Hack N’ Slash Historical game set 3,000 years ago in the times of King David’s reign. Currently I lead a team of six people working on the project, and it will be released in Spring 2026. I worked on project management, UI design, and gameplay programming.

Project Management

As Creative Director, I lead a team of six, directing the overall vision for the game while managing each department in terms of their quality. I oversee all aspects of the game’s development, including level design, gameplay mechanics, and artistic direction. I am in constant talks with the producer and leads, making sure the project vision is still in scope so that each department has a clear picture of their tasks for the week. While the producer makes sure that the team can accomplish goals in a specific amount of time, I make sure that all creative goals are met, having the final say on the result of what’s being made, including art, assets, mechanics, story, and more. I am also heavily involved in designing the documentation, including the GDD, ASG, Gantt charts, team contracts and more. Additionally, I am responsible for conducting playtesting sessions as development goes on, making playtesting reports and hearing feedback from testers and iterating that feedback into production. Gameplay Programming

Before we were into production, we first had to come up with a Proof of Concept to properly articulate what our game is supposed to be. Our team was given no more than two weeks to make a prototype build of our game showcasing our intended mechanics, art, and design that will represent the foundation our game is built from. I programmed all player movement and mechanics in this prototype, providing the foundation for player engagement and learning more about programming at a very fast pace. Here are some of my contributions for Gameplay Programming.

Movement and Blocking

Our game features 3D Third Person movement and the ability to Shield, Strafe, and Dodge. Using animation blend trees, I had to quickly figure out how to blend all these movements together to create seamless player movement. One obstacle I encountered was the Strafe. At first the animation wouldn’t play and would just slide since you need to be shielding while strafing and the shielding animation would override the strafing animation. So, I had to figure out a way to keep the top half of the shielding animation playing while the bottom half plays the strafing animation. After the Strafe was good, I moved onto Dodging, which involves a quick displacement of movement depending on the direction of the left control stick. I learned how to create a range function to play each dodge animation based on the determined range of inputs coming from the -/+ Y or X axis to ensure the player would dodge the correct way.

Lock On

Our game features a Lock On system that enables the player’s camera to orient towards an enemy when toggled. With the press of a button the player can engage Lock On if they are in range or disable it using the same button. They can also disable it when they move a certain distance away from the enemy. The Lock On mechanic uses a set of systems, including the first crucial function within the system, “finds closest target”, which works with the “find lock on target”, together they determine the proximity of a potential target. This coincides with the “Locked on Target” Boolean, which determines the target state and is initially set to false. If the system detects an enemy within the appropriate range, the camera rotation orients towards the enemy, and the “Locked on Target” Boolean sets to true.

Player Combat: Quick Attacks

For our prototype, we had to showcase two functioning combos available in our game, which are the Quick Attack and Strong Attack Combos respectively. The intention is that players string together sequences of attacks, creating distinct combos, with each combo finishing with a powerful ground or aerial attack. Quick Attacks are short and fast swings dealing decent damage to opponents. My process for creating the Quick Attack was this: the player presses the Quick Attack input, and the system checks the combo count. The combo count determines dmg amount and animation type. Each sequential input is incremental, chaining the quick attacks. The player uses the quick attack four consecutive times to deal max quick attack dmg. The fourth hit in this combo will initialize the combo’s finisher, dealing an automatic final blow to end the combo. A 1.5 second timer is fired for each time the player presses the Quick Attack input, giving them a short window of time before their combo resets.

Player Combat: Strong Attacks

Compared to Quicks, Strong Attacks involve slower, heavier swings that use more of the player’s momentum but do a lot of damage. My process for creating the Strong Attacks was similar to Quicks, since they are both groups of four and have a finisher, I just needed to change the damage output and adjust the animation array to find the Strong Attack animations instead of the Quicks, along with a few other things. The 1.5 second timer also applies to these combos as well.

Player Combat: Combining Quick and Strong Attacks

In our game, I wanted the player to be able to switch between Quick and Strong Attacks mid-combo, giving more freedom of expression and allowing more options for the player to experiment with, creating fun and engaging combat. For the prototype, I was able to achieve this goal and many others within the 2 week time frame we were given.
